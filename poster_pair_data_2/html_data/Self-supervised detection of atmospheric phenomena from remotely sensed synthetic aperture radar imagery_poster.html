Sentinel-1 ocean surface data and atmospheric stability

The European Space Agency provides unprecedented monitoring of Earth's oceans through a network of Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) satellites called Sentinel-1. Imagery from these satellites captures a variety of atmosphere and ocean surface phenomena including waves, atmospheric turbulence, ocean fronts, and marine biology.

The Sentinel-1 (S-1) satellites are equipped with a C-band synthetic aperture radar instrument to perform continuous high-resolution day-and-night imaging unaffected by cloud cover. One of the four acquisition modes of the SAR sensor, primarily for measuring ocean waves across the global open ocean, is the 'Wave' mode (WV).

Mesoscale convective cells, wind streaks, and negligible atmospheric variability are atmospheric phenomena representing a spectrum of stability regimes along the oceanic surface layer, corresponding to unstable, near stable, and stable conditions respectively.

Key insights:

  • We use deep learning to classify atmospheric phenomena in a hand-labeled dataset of synthetic aperture radar WaVe images.
  • We attempt to use contrastive learning on a dataset of 3 million unlabeled images to pretrain a model before finetuning on labeled data.
  • We show that the default setup of the SwAV contrastive learning framework does not outperform standard transfer learning from ImageNet weights.

Dataset

Unlabeled dataset. This dataset consists of three years (2017, 2018, 2019) of Copernicus S-1 A and B observations and contains 2,943,550 images, of which 90% are used for training and 10% for validation.

Labeled dataset. The labeled data is multi-label, allowing for multiple positive classes per WV image, and sampled randomly across the globe. Images were labeled by at least one expert and four classes of interest are used: (1) MC, (2) WS, (3) NV, and (4) other (OT). In total, we have 2,300 samples which are stratified and split into 60% training data, 20% validation data, and 20% held-out test data for final model evaluation.

Contrastive self-supervised pretraining

Contrastive self-supervised representation learning has demonstrated impressive representation learning capabilities on large amounts of unlabeled data.

Most modern contrastive learning methods are based on objectives that group representations of multiple views (obtained by applying different augmentations) of the same sample closely together while representations of other examples in the dataset are pushed away.

Example of the basic contrastive setup

Swapping Assignments between multiple Views (SwAV)[1], the contrastive framework used in this work, is based on a clustering objective, where representations are assigned to clusters and the network has to learn to predict cluster assignments.

Contrastive model evaluation

We used three common evaluation scenarios from the literature to compare contrastive weights to ImageNet weights:

  • k-nearest neighbors (kNN) evaluation of feature vectors
  • Linear evaluation of feature vectors
  • End-to-end finetuning of the entire ResNet50 network

Discussion and future direction

This work presents an initial exploration of self-supervised contrastive learning for satellite data. Our initial results do not support the use of contrastive learning for this application, with improvements over transfer learning from natural image features being marginal or not present at all, while requiring significant computation to train.

However, these results are preliminary, and more extensive evaluation and hyperparameter tuning may close the observed performance gap.

Our results, both with contrastive and ImageNet transfer learning, do show strong performance improvements over previous models for this task. This has immediate consequences for ocean surface layer estimation, where providing a highly reliable model can help with automated analysis and downstream remote sensing applications.
