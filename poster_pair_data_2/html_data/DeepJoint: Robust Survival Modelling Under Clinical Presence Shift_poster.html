Motivation

  • Observations' times and types reflect the patient's condition and the clinical staff's assessment of what was needed and when.
  • Predictive models have either partially leveraged this clinical presence process for improved performance or assumed it uninformative.
  • Although practitioners justify ignoring clinical presence to avoid leveraging a changeable and unpredictable process, no work has explored the impact of handling clinical presence on robustness.
  • Our work explores (i) how state-of-the-art methodologies behave under clinical presence shifts, and (ii) how to leverage clinical presence for improved transportability.

Methodology

We study in-ICU mortality prediction in MIMIC III leveraging laboratory tests after a 24-hour observation period.

Motivated by the difference in the counts of tests (Fig. 3) performed on patients admitted on weekends and weekdays, we hypothesise that practice might differ between weekends and weekdays.

  • Last - DeepSurv multi-layer perceptron relying on the last observation.
  • Count - Concatenate the 24-hour count of each observation to the last observation.
  • Ignore - An LSTM leveraging the data while ignoring its irregularity.
  • Resample - An LSTM with regularly resampled data.
  • GRU-D - Data concatenated with missingness indicators as inputs to a GRU-D.
  • Feature - An LSTM leveraging missingness and time elapsed since previous observation as inputs.
  • DeepJoint - The proposed methodology (Fig. 1) leveraging the same input as Feature.

Results

Figure 4 presents the transportability performance on patients admitted on weekdays measured through time-dependent C-index at three time horizons.

Insight 1 - Missingness and observation times are informative of short-term outcomes.

Insight 2 - Ignoring clinical presence might not only reduce performance but also hurt transportability.

Insight 3 - Explicit modelling of clinical presence improves performance and robustness.

Conclusions

Learning from medical data without sufficient consideration of the potential informativeness of clinical presence on outcome could impair performance and transportability.

We invite practitioners to:

  • Explore the practitioner-patient interaction process to identify clinical presence patterns.
  • Report the assumptions made on the clinical presence process.
  • Leverage informative observation process.
  • Evaluate transportability: internally and externally.
