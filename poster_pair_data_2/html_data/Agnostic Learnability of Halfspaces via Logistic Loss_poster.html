Setting.

Binary classification: given by distribution P on Rd × {-1, +1}; we have i.i.d. samples from P.

Linear classifier: x ↦ ⟨x, w⟩.

Goal: compete with the optimal solution ū, where

R0-1(ū) := Pr(x,y)~P (sign(⟨ū, x⟩) ≠ y) = OPT > 0.

Logistic regression (LR): let ℓlog(z) := ln(1 + e-z), and let

Rlog(w) := E(x,y)~P [ℓlog(y⟨w, x⟩)]

denote the population logistic risk of w over P.

Contributions.

Prior lower and upper bounds for LR do not match:
  • General P: 1 - OPT lower bound (Ben-David et al., 2012).
  • Isotropic log-concave distributions: Ω(OPT) lower bound (Diakonikolas et al., 2020).
  • "Well-behaved" sub-exponential distributions: O(√OPT) upper bound with SGD (Frei et al., 2021).
On "well-behaved" conditions:
  • Standard concentration and anti-concentration conditions.
  • Example: a mixture of log-concave dist. (e.g., Gaussian).
Our lower and upper bounds for LR:
  • Ω(√OPT) lower bound for well-behaved sub-exp dist.; matching O(√OPT) upper bound from (Frei et al., 2021).
  • O(OPT) upper bound with "radial Lipschitzness."
Upper bounds beyond LR:
  • Diakonikolas et al. (2020) designed a nonconvex SGD that achieves O(OPT) + ε risk using O(d/ε4) samples.
  • Other prior algorithms achieving O(OPT) + ε risk involve solving multiple rounds of convex program.
  • We design a simple two-phase convex program that achieves O(OPT ln(1/OPT)) + ε risk using O(d/ε2) samples, without radial Lipschitzness.

Lower bound.

We construct a distribution Q with three parts Q1, Q2, Q4.

  • 1. Q1 consists of two squares with edge length √OPT/2 and density 1; one has center (√2/2, -√2/2) and label -1, the other has center (-√2/2, √2/2) and label +1.
  • 2. Q2 is supported on [0, √OPT] × [0, 1] and [-√OPT, 0] × [-1, 0] with density 1, and the label is given by sign(x1).
  • 3. Q4 is the uniform distribution over the unit ball with density q4 and label sign(x1).

Theorem. Q is well-behaved and sub-exponential, and the global minimizer w* of Rlog has R0-1(w*) = Ω(√OPT).

Proof ideas:
  • To minimize Rlog on Q4, we should go to infinity along (1, 0).
  • Q1 keeps w* bounded with ||w*|| = O(1/√OPT).
  • Q2 drags w* above by an angle of Ω(√OPT).

Upper bound: radial Lipschitzness.

To exclude Q and overcome the lower bound, we make the following assumption for LR.

Radial Lipschitzness: There exists a measurable function κ : R+ → R+ such that for any two-dimensional subspace V, letting pV denote the density of the projection of feature distribution onto V, then

|pV(r, θ) - pV(r, θ')| ≤ κ(r)|θ - θ'|.
  • Holds if pV is Lipschitz, even non-log-concave ones (e.g., Gaussian mixtures).
  • Does not hold for general log-concave distributions.
  • Does not hold for Q, non-Lipschitz near the vertical axis.

Upper bound: logistic regression.

Projected gradient descent (PGD): let w0 := 0, and

wt+1 := ΠB(1/√ε) [wt - η∇R̂log(wt)].

Theorem. If the dist. is well-behaved, sub-exponential and radially-Lipschitz, with learning rate Õ(1/d), using poly(d, 1/ε, ln(1/δ)) samples and iterations, with prob. 1 - δ, PGD outputs wt with

R0-1(wt) = Õ(OPT) + ε.

Proof ideas: under the given conditions, suppose ŵ satisfies Rlog(ŵ) ≤ Rlog(||w̄||ū) + ε', then

R0-1(ŵ) = Õ ( max { OPT, √(ε' / ||w̄||) · (Cκ / ||w̄||2) } ).
  • Cκ = O(ln(1/OPT)2) for Lipschitz density.
  • The global optimizer w* of Rlog satisfies ||w*|| = Ω(1/√OPT); PGD also ensures wt has small ε' and ||wt|| = Ω(1/√OPT).

Upper bound: two-phase convex program.

Motivation: (1) holds for the hinge loss ℓh(z) := max{-z, 0} without radial Lipschitzness!

R0-1(ŵ) = Õ ( max { OPT, √(ε' / ||w̄||) } ).

However, the global minimizer of Rh is 0; if we find ŵ by global minimization, as ε' gets small, ||ŵ|| also gets small.

Ideas: LR followed by restricted Perceptron:
  • LR finds unit v that is Õ(√OPT) away from ū.
  • Then minimize Rh over D := {w | ⟨w, v⟩ ≥ 1}.
    • ∀w ∈ D, ||w|| ≥ 1.
    • ||w̄||ū may not in D, but (1 + Õ(OPT))||w̄||ū ∈ D!
  • Use SGD for better sample complexity.

Theorem. If the dist. is well-behaved and sub-exponential, using Õ(d/ε2) samples, SGD can achieve zero-one risk O(OPT ln(1/OPT)) + ε.
