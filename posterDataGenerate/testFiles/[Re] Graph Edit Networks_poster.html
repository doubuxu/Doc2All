Reproduced: Paaßen et. al. (ICLR, 2021)

The reproduced paper [bpa21] proposes a novel output layer for graph neural networks (GNNs), the graph edit network called (GEN). This layer yields a sequence of graph edits: node insertions, node deletions, node replacements, edge insertions and edge deletions.

The authors empirically underpin their theoretical claims about GENs by showing that they perform well in a series of graph time-series prediction tests.

DGPs & Tests

The paper evaluated several key claims on synthetic datasets, generated with data generating rules, interpreted in the following figures.

Graph cycles: A trivial set of user defined graph transformations without any logical rules.
Degree rules: A set of rules for node & edge insertions. (nodes with degree ≥ 3 are deleted; nodes with common neighbor are connected)
Conway's Game of Life: A series based on node edits on a lattice grid.
Boolean Formulae: Random Boolean formulae, simplified with rules like x ∧ ¬x → ⊥.
Peano Addition: Series of tree graphs, modelling addition problems via Peano's definition of addition.

[Re] Original experimental claims

[bpa21] makes three experimental conclusions based on a series of experiments. Checkmarks denote a successful reproduction of results.

  • ✓ GENs outperform baselines (Variational Graph Autoencoders & Variational Graph Recurrent Nets) on time-series prediction tasks.
  • ✓ GENs achieve 100% accuracy on time-series prediction tasks on graphs generated from Boolean Formulae & Peano Addition DGPs.
  • ? The runtime of forward passes of a GEN scales sub-quadratically as the number of nodes in a graph increases.
The runtime of graph scale dependence. Results for the backward passes are not consistent with the claim.
While the focus here is speed, we have to note that the experiment in the original paper fails to optimize the criterion loss.

Improving the experimental setup

The facts that graph-to-graph transitions are deterministic and that the space of unique graphs generated by DGPs is limited arose some concern.

We reproduced the claims with the following adjustments:

  • Increased cardinality of test time series (5 -> 100)
  • Ensured no duplicates between train & test set
  • Used established random graph generation methods

Results were generally worse, but still within a reasonable margin of error.

Concluding reproduction notes

Our experimental results conclusively show that most of the claims in the original work hold. However, our work beyond the original paper emphasizes the need to pay attention to the experimental setup, especially when working with synthetically generated data.
